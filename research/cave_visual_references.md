# CAVE visual reference research

## Verified official facility facts

Source: UCL, “Controlled Active Ventilation Environment”  
URL: https://www.ucl.ac.uk/engineering/civil-environmental-geomatic-engineering/facilities-cege/controlled-active-ventilation-environment

- CAVE means **Controlled Active Ventilation Environment**.
- It is a climate-controlled and ventilation-controlled indoor laboratory at UCL in Dagenham, London.
- The main hall has a **206 m² plan area** and a **9 m height**.
- The hall represents an exterior urban environment; full-scale buildings and vehicles can be placed or constructed inside it to create separately controlled indoor environments.
- The facility can accommodate full-scale living labs, including office/school-like rooms, facades and double-decker buses.
- Interior and exterior HVAC systems are independent.
- Exterior climate range: **−5 °C to 43 °C**. Interior climate range: **10 °C to 28 °C**.
- Both zones can receive fresh air, recirculated air or artificially generated pollutants.
- Monitoring includes temperature, humidity, airflow, CO₂, VOCs, particulate matter and NOx.
- The official gallery names four relevant views: “CAVE experiment room”, “cave vents”, “CAVE 3D rendering”, and “CAVE illustration”.

## Initial visual implications

- Do not portray CAVE as a sealed futuristic glass cube or a compact clean-room chamber.
- The dominant setting should be a **large, tall industrial test hall** containing a simpler full-scale room or facade as a test article.
- Show visible, oversized ventilation infrastructure and a clear distinction between the hall/exterior zone and the enclosed interior zone.
- Sensors should appear as believable mounted probes and instrument stands, not glowing sci-fi nodes.
- Any airflow overlay should be restrained and analytical, with independent exterior and interior paths.
- Maintain an architectural-section presentation, but base the section on the real hall-within-hall arrangement.

## Cross-checked facility geometry and appearance

The CIBSE Journal engineering account describes the handed-over facility as roughly **216 m² and 9.5 m high**, with more than **£300K of air-quality and airflow monitoring instruments** installed during commissioning. It reports early experiments on art-storage conditions and idling cars, and notes preparation for a full-scale two-storey modular building. These figures differ slightly from UCL’s rounded public specification of 206 m² and 9 m, so the visual should use the architect’s approximate internal geometry rather than printing a disputed exact dimension.

Source: https://www.cibsejournal.com/technical/from-covid-to-cave-ucls-living-lab/

Perkins&Will describes the principal laboratory as approximately **16 m wide × 16 m deep × 10 m high**. Its scale is set by temporary full-scale configurations: cellular offices, an airport lounge, a three-storey house and drive-in access for a double-decker bus. Natural ventilation can be represented by openable windows in the temporary construction, while the laboratory hall itself provides the separately controlled external environment. The official interior photograph shows a **very tall, bright white industrial hall**, not a compact glazed clean room. The hall uses pale insulated wall and ceiling surfaces, a smooth light floor, large service penetrations/ventilation openings and sparse movable test apparatus.

## Revised visual direction

The next hero should depict a tall white environmental hall containing a modest full-scale timber/metal-framed test room, with openable windows, mounted sensor stands, flexible duct connections and a small equipment rack. The exterior hall zone and interior room zone must be visually distinct. Dark editorial framing may remain outside the image, but the laboratory itself should stay bright, industrial and materially believable. Avoid black glass, luminous architecture, rooftop machinery on the test article, residential realism, decorative greenery and generic clean-room imagery.

## UCL brochure observations

Source: https://www.ucl.ac.uk/engineering/sites/engineering/files/ucl_cave_pearl.pdf

The official brochure confirms a deliberately utilitarian interior rather than a glossy technology showroom. The visible hall surface is matte white, formed from tall insulated-panel or lined wall bays. Galvanised metal service conduits run openly up the walls, with small red/yellow service points and safety markers. Large rectangular wall panels and access elements are flush or minimally detailed. This supports a bright industrial-hall depiction with exposed practical services, not a black cinematic chamber. The brochure’s third-page thumbnail appears to contain a plan/section drawing and should be inspected for the large-hall geometry before generating the replacement.

The brochure’s third page is a wide interior photograph rather than a plan. It shows a high matte-white wall with two plain industrial doors, exposed galvanised conduit rising vertically and bending into wall equipment, small rectangular ventilation grilles, and long low-level silver horizontal service/air-distribution assemblies protected by bright yellow end guards. The wall has large shallow rectangular access panels and sparse red triangular markers. The scene is functional, bright and almost entirely white/grey with safety-yellow accents. The accompanying text confirms **two building ventilation systems**, allowing the laboratory hall and a temporary experimental building inside it to have independent temperature and ventilation control. Fresh air, recirculated air or artificially generated pollutants can be supplied to either environment. The text repeats the approximate exterior range of −5 °C to 43 °C and interior range of 10 °C to 28 °C.

## Thermal-stratification experiment: verified system layout

Source: https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/id/eprint/10198604/1/IA2024_Extended_Abstract_LME.pdf

The 2024 Indoor Air paper describes CAVE as a fully insulated chamber of about **204 m²** with a **9.54 m floor-to-ceiling height**. Four dedicated AHUs can create different internal environments. Air can be supplied at **high and low levels** and extracted at both levels; supply temperature is controlled through VAVs with localised heating coils. The BMS sensing is supplemented by **82 Sensirion Explora loggers** distributed around the laboratory, reporting temperature and relative humidity every two minutes. The paper also describes a vertical measurement profile on the north wall from approximately 0.5 m to 9.5 m.

For the stratification experiment, the low-level supply delivered approximately **1,260 L/s at 10.5 °C through 12 outlets**, while the high-level supply delivered approximately **1,260 L/s at 28 °C through 12 outlets**. Four extracts at each level balanced the supplies. The AHU operated in recirculation-only mode for that experiment. The published schematic depicts a simple large rectangular hall with red and blue high/low supply bands around the perimeter, not a dense ceiling-duct maze. This supports showing restrained colour-coded airflow traces and a few precise wall-mounted sensing points rather than decorative holographic particles.

## Additional engineering/construction cross-checks

Etec Associates confirms the two independently controlled environments, generator backup and use of adjacent photovoltaic generation. Source: https://www.etec.me.uk/projects/cave/

VolkerFitzpatrick confirms a large reconfigurable experiment area with observation/preparation spaces, lab benches, write-up spaces, thermal walling and plant sized for the laboratory load. Source: https://www.volkerfitzpatrick.co.uk/en/projects/ucl-cave-research-laboratory

The VolkerFitzpatrick page contains a clearly implausible rendered temperature range of “−50C to +400C”, which conflicts with UCL’s official −5 °C to 43 °C range and is excluded from the visual specification.
